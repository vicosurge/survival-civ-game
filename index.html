Cambrera

The old continent burned.

A war no one wanted, waged for reasons long since forgotten, unmade the land our grandparents called home. Those who escaped scattered — most to the southern mainland, where another people, kin in shape but not in tongue, made room for them in cities already too full.

Others looked north. Past the grey straits, to a small island the old charts named Cambrera (cam-BREH-rah). The soil is what it is, and the winters bite. But the land is unclaimed, and quiet, and what we raise here will be ours — raised by our hands, for the first time.

This is a world of iron axes and ploughshares, of timber halls and stone keeps. The great empires that built the roads are dust, and the old magics with them — shrines stand silent, and the songs of the elder days grow shorter every generation. What remains is earth, timber, and the patience to work both.

You arrive with five souls, two children, and the ship that brought you. Break ground. Keep them fed. Build something worth staying for.
