Cambrera

The old continent burned.

A war no one wanted, waged for reasons long since forgotten, unmade the land our grandparents called home. Those who escaped scattered — most to the southern mainland, where another people, kin in shape but not in tongue, made room for them in cities already too full.

Others looked north. Past the grey straits, to a small island the old charts named Cambrera. It is modest as mainlands go, and the soil is what it is. But it is unclaimed, and quiet, and whatever we raise here will be ours — raised for the first time, by us.

This is a world of iron axes and ploughshares, of timber halls and stone keeps. The great empires that built the roads are dust; what little they called magic has faded with them. What remains is earth, timber, and the patience to work both.

You arrive with five souls, two children, and the ship that brought you. Break ground. Keep them fed. Build something worth staying for.
